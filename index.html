BETWEEN JOY AND TURMOIL: A WEEK IN SEVEN LIVES

THE NEXT GENERATION: WHEN CHILDREN FORGE THEIR OWN BONDS

Children in bubble bath - Photo by Nimrod Ronen

When Nimrod Ronen and his daughter Alma visited Avi Yagil's family for a second consecutive weekend, something profound emerged beneath the surface of their friendship.

In an ordinary yet extraordinary moment, the children played together in a bubble bath, creating a scene that struck both fathers as deeply symbolic.

"Looking at our children taking a foam bath together was a moving and formative moment," reflects Nimrod. "The children are planting another future for our friendship, as if not only were we having children, but the relationship itself has a second generation."

The image of children continuing friendships their parents began decades ago stands in stark relief against the backdrop of uncertainty permeating daily life in Israel. The photos of Alma and Dori playing represent hope and continuity in a place where both can feel precarious.

Avi agrees, noting the moment "was really touching to see them like that," adding with characteristic humor that the resulting images "looked like something from a Shilav catalog."

In a week filled with professional obligations, political tensions, and parenting challenges, this shared moment between children represented something both fathers treasured: the prospect that the connections they've built might outlast themselves.

LIVING IN ISRAEL'S DUAL REALITY

To exist in Israel in March 2025 is to become expert in cognitive dissonance. The increasingly schizophrenic split between personal joy and national dread manifested in moments both mundane and profound.

"Then I went to defecate and opened the news and saw that the genocide they supposedly put on 'pause' had returned in full force. So I went back to the room and looked at my son again and promised myself I'd get him out of here as soon as possible."

— Ben Ronen

Ben Ronen captured this duality most viscerally Thursday morning, when he woke at 4:30 a.m. to find his son sleeping peacefully beside him. "I looked at my son sleeping next to me and it filled me with happiness," he shared, along with a photo of his son in costume.

Moments later, while checking the news, this domestic tranquility shattered. The whiplash between these mental states—from parental bliss to existential dread in minutes—exemplifies the emotional gymnastics required by daily life.

Ben's son in costume - Photo by Ben Ronen

Earlier in the week, Avi experienced similar psychic disruption after navigating a typical Monday of work and childcare. He found himself automatically opening the news: "The headline is Netanyahu fired Ronen Bar, and again reality here slaps you in the face, that this place is sick and spiraling downward, and there's no strength left for anything, not to change reality, not to eat something tasty."

By week's end, Nimrod articulated what many seemed to be feeling: "The volume of this schizophrenic place keeps rising. Against my relatively peaceful life here with its small worries and pleasures are piles of guilt, anger, confusion wrapped in lots of repression." He concluded with a question that haunts many parents: "How will I explain this to my children?"

WELLNESS & LIFESTYLE

WATER, YOGA, AND CLIMBING WALLS: PHYSICAL WELLNESS AS MENTAL REFUGE

In a world where control seems increasingly elusive, the friends turned to their bodies as domains they could still partially govern. Through swimming, yoga, bouldering, and dedicated posture work, they found momentary respites from both physical pain and psychological distress.

For Nimrod, Monday's swim training represented his most cherished weekly ritual. "The best moment of my week is when I swim in the pool during training," he writes. "I come out exhausted and strengthened. Yesterday they even held a sprint competition and I gave it my all."

"Water has always been the thing that relaxes me most in the world."

— Nimrod Ronen

Nimrod at swimming pool - Photo by Nimrod Ronen

Avi likewise found temporary sanctuary in a yoga class at the workplace club he established. "In the sports and back spirit of today, I went to do yoga at the yoga club I established at work, and the things were somewhat forgotten for a moment between right twist and happy baby," he writes, though quickly adding, "but still the reality is shocking."

Avi's yoga session - Photo by Avi Yagil

Meanwhile, in Seattle, Daniel has been exploring bouldering since the beginning of the year. "It's a bit interesting and troubling and quite amazing," he reports, noting with characteristic dry humor that sometimes he feels "I'll break my back this way someday."

The week also featured an extended group discussion about back pain management, with particular focus on the Ilan Lev method. Ofer Tisser reports "great improvement" after two years of treatment, while Ben similarly recommends the technique. Omri's first visit to a gym specifically for back strengthening sparked the conversation, suggesting that bodily well-being remains a common concern—and a topic where the friends readily offer support.

FAMILY & RELATIONSHIPS

DAWN WITH THE LITTLE ONES: PARENTING'S EARLIEST HOURS

The crack of dawn finds many of these friends already awake—not by choice, but by parental obligation or professional necessity. Yet within these early hours, moments of profound connection emerge.

Nimrod reports his daughter "woke up at five in the morning and insisted on putting on socks by herself, a motor skill stage she has completely mastered, and I think I still haven't." By six, he was already "eating Lego ice cream, Lego coffee, and Lego hot chocolate" with her.

Early morning play with daughter - Photo by Nimrod Ronen

These pre-dawn parenting moments often yield unexpected tenderness. Ben's Thursday morning awakening at 4:30 allowed him to observe his sleeping son—a moment of pure contentment before the interruption of news alerts and work responsibilities. The promise he subsequently made to himself—"to get him out of here as soon as possible"—underscores how these intimate family moments often crystallize parents' priorities amid uncertainty.

Even pickup routines become memorable. Avi shares how after leaving a work event, "I cut out to Dori's daycare because it's my pickup day, and there's nothing like that moment of taking him from daycare when he runs to you excitedly when you arrive." He had brought a panda toy to surprise his son, "but he threw it and gave a hug just to me."

PARENTING BRIEFS

  • Avi's son chose his father over a panda toy during daycare pickup
  • Nimrod and partner discussed timing for having another child while enjoying rare quiet time
  • Daniel and family continued fertility treatments, with their "third and likely final egg retrieval"
ARTS & CULTURE

COLLECTIVE ESCAPISM: THE SHOWS AND SONGS GETTING FRIENDS THROUGH THE WEEK

As national tensions escalate, cultural consumption becomes more than entertainment—it transforms into necessary psychological refuge. The friends engaged actively with literature, music, and television throughout the week, often using these media as temporary escape hatches from reality.

Television proved particularly important as common ground. Multiple members discussed current seasons of "Wedding Night" (חתונמי), which Ofer described as "the toughest season since COVID." The conversation revealed shared investment in the show's characters, with Nimrod noting one episode was "really intense" while describing a particular character as "painfully cringe at insane levels."

"Severance" also emerged as a group favorite, though reactions to its second season were mixed. "From the first season I really fell hard for it," Ofer explained, before admitting disappointment that "this season really declined, all the packaging remained very impressive but the script simply became just another sci-fi series."

Books provided more solitary escape. Ben highly recommended Werner Herzog's "Dusk World," describing it as "a very special book" about a Japanese Imperial Army lieutenant who refused to surrender until 1974—29 years after World War II ended. This literary enthusiasm contrasted with his assessment of the prog rock album it inspired: "I tried to listen to the album which is really so bad. You know the tip of the garbage bag that drips with liquid? Well, exactly that."

Record store visit - Photo by Avi Yagil

By week's end, Avi reported visiting the relocated "Dune" record store, now operating "for the third time in a warehouse above Uganda," where he met a friend for coffee. He observed it was "nice to be in another world where every moment crazy or cute people come in looking for music."

This cultural curation—finding the right shows, songs, and stories to process or temporarily forget current realities—emerged as critical emotional infrastructure for the group.

WORK & DAILY LIFE

FLOWERING AMID CHAOS: FINDING PEACE IN DAILY WORK

For some members of the group, professional routines provide structure and occasional beauty amid disorder. This proved particularly true for Ben, whose early morning work with flowers at Danziger Flowers' cultivation division offers both literal and metaphorical distance from chaos.

"Morning glimpse from my work that starts very early," Ben writes, sharing an image from his flower operation. "Flowers and working with them manage to filter much of the sewage flowing around us."

Ben's flower operation - Photo by Ben Ronen

Work environments vary dramatically across the group. While Ben begins his days in agricultural serenity, Daniel codes at Amazon's offices in Seattle. Though physically distant from Israel's immediate tensions, Daniel's international perspective brings different challenges—including the cultural adjustment of parenting in America, where his son's previous babysitter "left the profession and/or the country."

The group's diverse professional roles—spanning entertainment, agriculture, marketing, gaming, and technology—reveal the breadth of their experiences while highlighting a common thread: five of the seven members hold management positions, prompting Ben to note early in the week, "so far 4/4 managers," before Ofer added, "wait, actually me too, head of drama development at Gil Productions!"

This professional success stands in sometimes uncomfortable contrast to national anxieties—competence in controlled work environments alongside powerlessness in the broader political sphere.

EDITORIAL

A WEEK IN FRAGMENTS

What emerges from this experiment—documenting daily experiences that would later become a newspaper—is a portrait of friendship as both witness and refuge. Against the backdrop of political turmoil and parental responsibility, these seven friends continue finding meaning in shared cultural references, wellness strategies, and the simple act of telling each other what happened today.

The contrasts are stark: the peace of a child sleeping against renewed conflict, the joy of professional accomplishment against national decline, the pleasure of entertainment against the burden of awareness. Yet within these contradictions, connection persists.

As the week concluded, the group unanimously voted to continue this documentation project, finding value in transforming everyday experiences into shared narrative. Perhaps this itself is an act of resistance: insisting that personal moments matter, that friendship endures, that joy remains possible—even as, and perhaps especially because, reality continues to "slap you in the face."
